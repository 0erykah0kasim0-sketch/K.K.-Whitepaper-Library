## English Abstract

Small and medium-sized islands increasingly face compressed and potentially catastrophic security risks. Conventional debates tend to focus on arms buildup, deterrence posture, and external support, while paying relatively little attention to the active role of natural environments in shaping strategic outcomes. Yet island-specific featuresâ€”mountainous terrain, ocean currents, turbulent wind fields, and dense urban micro-climatesâ€”exhibit inherent chaotic properties that are difficult to fully predict or control. These dynamics do not merely â€œcomplicate operationsâ€; under high-speed, high-precision conditions they can systematically alter the certainty of terminal effects.
This article introduces the concepts of Island Natural Terrain Resilience and the Natural Denial Operating System (ND-OS). It argues that islands can treat natural chaos as a low-cost, low-visibility layer of passive denial, one that works not by intercepting attacks but by reducing the decisiveness and interpretability of first moves. Through terrain occlusion, flow-induced drift, sensing disruptions, and cognitive gaps in effect assessment, ND-OS can transform putative â€œdecisive first blowsâ€ into ambiguous outcomes that require additional verification, thereby increasing buffer time for both island societies and the broader international community.
The paper first analyzes key natural features of islands and explains how they complicate external sensing, planning, and execution. It then explores how natural chaos creates terminal drift, micro-timing deviations, and decision-making hesitation, weakening the escalation logic of â€œquick, clean victory.â€ Building on this analysis, the article outlines a three-layer ND-OS framework and discusses its implications for island security planning. Finally, it offers policy recommendations regarding integrated observation platforms, buffer-time-oriented planning metrics, nature-based strategic literacy, and cross-disciplinary cooperation. The ND-OS concept aims to provide a low-cost yet potentially high-impact pathway to enhance strategic survivability for vulnerable islands.
